# GM Guidance

> [!GM] This section is for the GM only. Players don't need to read it — but it won't ruin anything if they do.

---

## Running Combat & Pacing

Combat in MDM is fast and dangerous. Keep it that way.

**Your job in combat is momentum.** Don't let it stall. If a player is thinking too long, give them a 10-second count and move on — they can always act next round.

**Monster turns are simple by design.** Mooks act as a group. Roll once, apply results. Don't give them complex tactics unless it serves the scene. They're there to create pressure, not puzzles.

**Telegraphing danger is fair play.** If an enemy is about to do something brutal, hint at it. *The orc raises its axe in a two-handed grip.* Players should feel like they had a chance to react, even when they didn't take it.

### Pacing by Encounter Size

| Enemies | Tone | Target Length |
|---------|------|--------------|
| 1-2 | Skirmish — fast, decisive | 2-3 rounds |
| 3-5 | Real threat — positioning matters | 4-6 rounds |
| Boss fight | Slow burn — escalating danger | As long as it needs |

**0 HP isn't always death.** Unconscious PCs bleed out — that's a clock, not a verdict. Other PCs can stabilize them. Make that choice feel real and costly.

**End combat before it drags.** If the outcome is clearly decided, let the last mook flee or surrender. A battle that's already won doesn't need three more rounds of cleanup.

## NPC & Social Encounters

Social mechanics exist to add stakes, not replace roleplay. Don't call for a roll just because a player is talking to someone.

### When to Roll

- The NPC has a reason to resist or distrust
- The stakes are real if it fails
- The player is attempting something beyond normal conversation

### When NOT to Roll

- The NPC would freely give the information
- Good roleplay already did the work — reward it, don't tax it
- The outcome doesn't matter either way

### Disposition

NPCs have a starting disposition toward the PCs ranging from **-4 (hostile)** to **+3 (friendly)**. This modifies social roll difficulty.

| Disposition | Modifier | Meaning |
|-------------|----------|---------|
| +3 | -3 to roll target (easier) | Friendly, wants to help |
| +1/+2 | -1/-2 | Warm, open |
| 0 | No modifier | Neutral |
| -1/-2 | +1/+2 | Suspicious, wary |
| -3/-4 | +3/+4 (harder) | Hostile, adversarial |

**LK and first impressions.** High LK PCs get natural warmth — strangers give them benefit of the doubt. Low LK PCs read as off somehow. Play this subtly. It's not magic, it's vibes.

### Social Outcomes

| Result | Outcome |
|--------|---------|
| Strong Success | NPC goes above and beyond, may offer extra |
| Success | NPC does what was asked |
| Partial | NPC does part of it, or with conditions |
| Failure | NPC refuses — try a different angle |
| Botch | NPC is offended or alarmed — word may spread |

**Failure isn't a wall.** A failed roll means "not like that, not right now." Give players a different angle — information they could find, leverage they could gain, someone else they could approach first.

**Let NPCs want things.** The best social encounters happen when the NPC has their own agenda. Find the overlap or the friction between what they want and what the PCs want.

## Handling Fate Points & LK

Fate Points are the player's safety valve — don't be stingy with awarding them, but don't let them feel trivial.

**Award Fate Points for:**
- Clever solutions that change the encounter
- Meaningful roleplay that costs the character something
- Moments of genuine heroism or sacrifice
- Making the table laugh (GM's discretion)

**LK in practice:**
- Don't announce when LK is affecting things — just let it shape outcomes
- Low LK characters should feel like bad things trend toward them; not always, just often enough
- High LK characters get small advantages in ambiguous moments — the door is unlocked, the guard is distracted, the rain holds off

---

## Difficulty & When to Call for Rolls

**The golden rule:** Only roll when the outcome is uncertain AND failure has consequences.

If a trained character is doing something routine in a non-stressful situation, they succeed. No roll.

If the outcome is guaranteed regardless of the roll, skip it.

**Reserve rolls for:**
- Dangerous situations
- Time pressure
- Active opposition
- High personal stakes

> [!GM] Calling for too many rolls makes the system feel like a slot machine. Calling for too few makes skills irrelevant. Find the middle ground: rolls should feel meaningful when they happen.
